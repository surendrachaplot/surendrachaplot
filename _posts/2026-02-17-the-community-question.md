I've been thinking about what "community-owned" actually means.

Not the marketing version of it — the real version. The one with teeth.

There's a venue in Catford called Sister Midnight. They took over an old working men's club, got a 10-year lease at peppercorn rent from the council, and turned it into the first community-owned music space in Lewisham. The whole thing started because a woman called Lenny was writing her dissertation about why music culture was dying, noticed a venue she loved was about to become a cheese and wine bar, and thought: *well, shit, maybe I should do something instead of just writing about it.*

That's the part I keep coming back to.

Because I'm doing something similar with The Club. And the easiest thing in the world would be to build it the normal way — find an investor, sign a lease in my name, hire staff, open doors. That's the model everyone understands. Investors understand it. Banks understand it. Estate agents understand it.

Community ownership is harder to explain. You have to describe co-ops, and community shares, and governance structures, and people's eyes glaze over. And then you have to convince people to buy in — not to a product, but to an idea — before anything exists.

But here's the thing. I don't actually want to own a nightclub. I want to be part of one. I want to build something that belongs to the people who show up, not to whoever put in the most money. And I think that's the only version worth building.

Sister Midnight did it. The council gave them the keys because they had a community around them before they had a building. They'd been proving the concept in a tiny Deptford record shop for two years before the pandemic shut them down. By the time they needed a space, people were already invested in what they were doing.

That's the playbook. The space comes second. The people come first.

So right now, while I'm still in Singapore, I'm not looking for buildings. I'm asking a different question: who are the people who want this? Who would show up every Saturday? Who would run a workshop on a Wednesday morning? Who feels like there's no place in London that's quite theirs?

When I have a clear picture of that community — their faces, their names, what they're making, what they need — *then* the building question gets easier.

In the meantime, I'm reading everything I can about the Sister Midnight model. About what grants exist. About which councils are actively looking for community anchors. About what the co-op structure actually looks like when you do it properly.

Lewisham is interesting. They designated a Creative Enterprise Zone covering New Cross, Deptford, Catford. They gave Sister Midnight peppercorn rent because it fit what the council was trying to do for the borough. That's a lever.

There are probably five or six boroughs in London doing similar things right now. The trick is finding which council is most motivated, which spaces are available, and whether the community you're building matches what they need.

That's the research I'm doing this week. Results to follow.
